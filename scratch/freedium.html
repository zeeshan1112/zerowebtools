Prologue: The Whisper That Shaped a Species

Homo sapiens did not conquer the planet because individual humans acted faster than other animals but because human groups could coordinate better, more flexibly, and at a greater scale. Such complex coordination, so characteristic of humans, depends on a dynamic flow of social understanding. It concerns how alliances are formed, who betrays trust, who acts generously or cruelly, and who proves competent or incompetent.

At the latest by the evolutionary caesura of the Cognitive Revolution, roughly 70,000 years ago, and against the background of language capacities that may have been present by about 100,000 to 135,000 years ago, this social understanding began to take the form of stories, reputations, and expectations moving from human to human and from mind to mind.

Across millennia of human social and cultural development, this communicative practice expanded and grew more complex. It moved from the intimate economies of small bands, where it tracked reputations, punished defectors, and rewarded cooperators, to salons, newspapers, radio, platforms, and now machine-mediated global channels, where even artificial intelligence agents exchange second-hand claims about other agents, third parties, sources, and reputations.

At the center of the long history of this communicative practice stands gossip, a form of speech about absent others that often carries a poor reputation in moral discourse and polite society but, from an anthropological point of view, belongs among the most evolutionarily consequential forms of human collective communication. It persists with extraordinary ease across cultures and historical periods because it answers a recurrent social need.

One reason for this remarkable persistence may lie in how frequently humans gather and exchange information about absent others—who did what, who will do what, who can be trusted, and who cannot.

The British biological anthropologist and evolutionary psychologist R. I. M. Dunbar argues that roughly two-thirds of human conversation concerns social topics that fall broadly under the category of gossip. ⇱

Frequency may explain why gossip is so visible in ordinary conversation, but its durability becomes clearer in Dunbar's comparison with primate social grooming. ⇱ In primate groups, grooming maintains social bonds through physical touch, whereas language allows humans to maintain and extend social cohesion through reputational information that can circulate far beyond direct bodily contact. Gossip is essential to this transition because it strengthens alliances, helps enforce norms at low cost, and makes violations socially visible beyond one-to-one interaction.

The anthropological argument has a simple mathematical implication. If group size grows while individual time remains fixed, social cohesion becomes a problem of communicative capacity.

It is possible to formalize Dunbar's scaling claim as a simple capacity inequality. Let N denote group size, and let each individual have a fixed social-maintenance time budget τ per day. Suppose that maintaining one relationship through grooming is dyadic and costs c****₁ units of time per partner, so one person can actively maintain at most d₁=τ/c****₁ relationships per day, up to rounding. By contrast, suppose that a gossip or talk episode costs c****₂ units of time but reaches, on average, s(N****) distinct partners per episode, so the effective capacity becomes d₂=(τ/c₂)·s(N****). If cohesion in a group of size N requires each person to keep at least a fixed fraction ρ of possible ties "warm," meaning d≥ρ·(N−1****), then grooming yields a hard size ceiling τ/c₁≥ρ·(N****−1), or equivalently, N≤1+τ/(ρ·c****₁).

It shifts social maintenance from a strictly one-to-one mechanism with bounded capacity under fixed τ to a one-to-many mechanism whose effective capacity can increase through the amplification factor s(N****).

The capacity model shows why this is important. Beyond its possible moral or psychological satisfactions, gossip addresses a deeper scaling problem because group size can grow while each person's daily social-maintenance time remains bounded. Under that condition, any communicative mechanism that reaches several partners within the same amount of time gains a structural advantage. Dunbar's "grooming to gossip" thesis can therefore be understood as a scaling transition because language, once it supplements touch, changes how many social ties can be maintained under the same time constraint. ⇱

In short, gossip changes the arithmetic of social cohesion.

A second perspective concerns what gossip does within the minds of individuals and within the moral memory of the group. Gossip may have a modest stabilizing effect at the psychological level, not because it simply reduces stress, but because it can clarify norms, convert uncertainty into shared interpretation, and strengthen an individual's sense of social orientation and group belonging. ⇱

This psychological and cultural layer helps explain why gossip is more than the circulation of loose information. It can function as a form of cultural learning because it encodes norms in narratives about concrete people and concrete consequences. Baumeister, Zhang, and Vohs argue that gossip teaches people how to live within a culture by relaying norm violations and their social penalties in story form. ⇱ Foster's taxonomy review broadens this view by organizing gossip across social, evolutionary, and personal functions, which helps explain why a behavior with a morally "bad" label can still produce value at the level of the group. ⇱ The remaining question is why a behavior that may contribute to group cohesion so often receives a negative moral reputation. Hartung and colleagues add a modern empirical layer, showing that gossip serves motives ranging from information exchange and bonding to self-enhancement, while its moral reputation often diverges from its typical social role. ⇱

This essay does not claim to invent a new mathematics of gossip from nothing. It offers a mathematical reading of gossip by bringing together tools that already exist in rumor modeling, network theory, information theory, game theory, point processes, distributed computing, and recommender-system analysis. Whenever a claim about an absent third party moves from one agent to another, it can be treated as a formal object with content, carriers, transmission paths, credibility, incentives, distortion, and reach. The same elements recur in face-to-face conversation, mass media, social platforms, distributed computer systems, and modern AI systems, although each medium changes the speed, cost, topology, and failure modes of transmission.

1. A Rumor on a Network and the Classical Daley–Kendall Model

A mathematical reading of gossip begins by reducing the phenomenon to its minimal formal structure. Let G=(V,E****) represent a social network. A vertex i∈V represents a human agent, and an edge (i,j)∈****E represents a communication channel through which a message can pass from i to j.

A rumor then has two separable components. It has content, represented by a message m∈𝓜, and it has a state, represented by each agent's relation to that message. In reality, an agent may be unaware, aware but inactive, aware and active, skeptical, committed, or already tired of repeating it. The simplest models, however, suppress the internal structure of m and treat the rumor as an index whose spread can be tracked. Later models can allow the content to mutate, measure semantic drift, or even couple transmission to incentives and algorithmic curation.

Given an initial set of informed agents A₀⊆V, what law governs the set Aₜ⊆V of agents aware of the rumor at time t, and what determines the final reach A*?

Foundational rumor models answer this question by treating rumor spread as an epidemic-like process. In 1964, Daley and Kendall gave this analogy one of its foundational mathematical forms, now known as the Daley–Kendall (DK) rumor model. ⇱

Their rumor model and its later variants partition a population into three compartments. Ignorants I(t) have not heard the rumor. Spreaders S(t) know it and actively transmit it. Stiflers R(t) know it but no longer spread it.

This stifling mechanism differs significantly from the epidemic picture. In a disease model, contact with an immune individual may block transmission without changing the infected person's state. In the DK rumor model, contact with an already informed listener can change the teller's state from spreader to stifler.

Through the quadratic term –𝛼s(s+r), the model represents, in compact form, the loss of novelty and social value as the rumor saturates the population. As the informed population grows, spreaders increasingly meet people who already know the rumor, and those encounters reduce the number of active tellers. In the DK rumor model, the process can therefore burn out before the rumor reaches everyone. Maki and Thompson's 1973 monograph later placed this family of rumor models within a broader mathematical modeling framework and became a canonical reference for the early systematic treatment of rumor dynamics. ⇱

The Daley–Kendall family gives three durable lessons. First, rumor spread is self-limiting even without recovery in the epidemiological sense because saturation increases stifling encounters. Second, its final reach usually remains below the whole population under standard assumptions because spreaders may stop before all ignorants have been contacted. Third, parameters that raise transmission to ignorants, such as a high 𝛽, or reduce stifling, such as a low 𝛼, increase reach, although network topology can become decisive once the mean-field assumption fails.

Modern work revisits this paradigm under more realistic assumptions, including heterogeneous activity, explicit network structure, credibility, hesitation, and belief. Chen and Wang, for example, extend the classical compartment approach by introducing rumor credibility and life-correlation parameters, subdividing ignorants by personality type, adding an exposed or hesitating class, and deriving spreading thresholds through next-generation-matrix analysis on homogeneous and heterogeneous networks. ⇱

2. Network Structure from Small Worlds to Heterogeneous Mean-Field Theory

Mean-field models, such as the Daley–Kendall rumor model, coarse-grain the explicit contact network into a homogeneous-mixing approximation in which interaction rates depend on population averages rather than on individual connections. This is useful as a first approximation, but it discards the graph-topological information that determines the paths through which gossip travels.

Real social networks are not smooth populations. Some ties form dense local clusters, some bridge distant groups, some individuals have far more connections than others, and some groups form internally dense regions with fewer external links. These properties are known respectively as clustering, bridging, degree heterogeneity, and community structure.

Once these features matter, the same number of spreaders can produce different outcomes depending on their positions in the graph. A rumor inside a tight cluster may circulate without escaping, while the same rumor near a bridge or a high-degree node may cross into distant parts of the network.

Watts and Strogatz, for example, showed that if a small fraction of edges in a sparse network act as long-range shortcuts, the network can retain high local clustering while acquiring short characteristic path lengths. ⇱ This is the small-world range, in which local neighborhoods remain dense while distant regions become reachable in relatively few steps. ⇱

"Ordinarily, the connection topology is assumed to be either completely regular or completely random. But many biological, technological and social networks lie somewhere between these two extremes." ⇱

The key point is that these two quantities respond differently to small changes in p. The characteristic path length L(p) falls sharply once a few long-range shortcuts appear because distant parts of the graph become reachable in fewer steps. The clustering coefficient C(p), however, remains comparatively high over the same range because most local neighborhoods are still intact. The network remains locally clustered, but it no longer behaves like a large regular lattice at a global scale.

For rumor propagation, the consequence is clear. Clustering keeps gossip circulating within local circles, while shortcut ties reduce the number of steps needed to reach distant parts of the network.

Degree heterogeneity adds a second structural correction. In a homogeneous mean-field model, every individual is treated as if they had roughly the same number of contact opportunities. In a heterogeneous network, however, some nodes have many more edges than others. These high-degree nodes therefore have more opportunities to receive and transmit a rumor, so transmission can concentrate around them and proceed more rapidly.

Moreno, Nekovee, and Pacheco formalize this correction for rumor spreading on complex networks by deriving mean-field equations conditioned on degree class and comparing homogeneous networks with random scale-free networks. ⇱ Let iₖ(t****), sₖ(t****), and rₖ(t****) denote the fractions of degree-k nodes in the ignorant, spreader, and stifler compartments.

In the simulations reported by Moreno, Nekovee, and Pacheco, the final density of stiflers is essentially independent of the initial seed's degree, while the time needed to reach that value decreases as the degree of the seed increases.

Degree heterogeneity explains why some starting points accelerate rumor spread, but it does not by itself answer when local transmission remains confined and when it develops into a network-wide cascade. Percolation theory gives one mathematical answer to this question.

Newman showed that, for suitable SIR-like processes on networks, final outbreak size can be mapped to bond percolation through a transmissibility parameter T. ⇱ His key move is to replace the temporal spreading process with a static connectivity problem. Suppose each edge has probability T of successfully carrying transmission during the active period. We keep that edge with probability T and delete it otherwise. The remaining graph is the percolated graph. The final outbreak from a seed is the set of nodes connected to that seed in this reduced graph. If the reduced graph contains a giant connected component, a single local seed can reach a macroscopic fraction of the network.

For gossip, the same reasoning can be applied with care. A gossip transmissibility does not measure infection but the effective probability that a contact occurs, the rumor is passed on, the recipient pays attention, and the recipient becomes a spreader rather than an immediate stifler. Under suitable independence assumptions, the final informed set can then be approximated by a percolation cluster. In this sense, a rumor becomes viral when the effective transmission process crosses the threshold at which the reachable component becomes large.

The simulation visualizes why virality is a threshold feature. For small T_g, open edges form isolated local islands, so the seed can reach only a small neighborhood. Near the threshold, separate components begin to merge, and a modest increase in T_g can suddenly expand the reachable set. Above the threshold, the seed connects to a large component, so a local rumor can reach a macroscopic fraction of the network. For gossip, T_g should be understood as an effective probability that includes contact, attention, retelling, and the chance that the recipient becomes a spreader rather than an immediate stifler.

Percolation theory explains when a rumor can become reachable across a large part of the graph, but reachability is not yet the same as endorsement or repetition. Some rumors spread after a single exposure, while others require social confirmation before an agent believes them strongly enough to pass them on. Granovetter's threshold model formalizes this second regime at the aggregate level. In his original formulation, each actor has a threshold, meaning the number or proportion of others who must already have made a decision before that actor does so. For rumors, he notes explicitly that people vary in credulity and that some may need to hear a rumor from more than one person before they believe it strongly enough to spread it.

In the aggregate model, if a(t****) denotes the proportion of actors who have already adopted and F is the cumulative distribution of thresholds, the update rule is a(t+1)=F[a****(t)].

This is a powerful equation because it shows how collective outcomes can depend sensitively on the exact distribution of thresholds rather than merely on average preferences. Granovetter's own examples show that two populations with nearly identical threshold distributions can generate radically different outcomes when a small gap appears in the sequence of thresholds.

3. Information Theory and Gossip as Noisy Transmission and Semantic Drift

The previous models track who has been reached by a rumor, but they do not yet track what happens to the rumor itself. This matters because gossip is almost never repeated without alteration. A story may preserve part of its central claim while losing context, gaining emphasis, acquiring moral color, or becoming more certain than the evidence allows.

This inequality says that a retelling cannot, by itself, increase the mutual information between the message and the underlying truth. It may inflate the claim, sharpen its emphasis, or increase apparent confidence, but those changes do not count as reliable information unless an external source enters the chain. Repeated circulation can also stabilize a claim socially, turning it into a pseudo-fact that a group treats as shared knowledge because it has been repeated and mutually confirmed rather than independently verified.

This does not mean that every rumor must lose all informational content. Redundancy, correction, cross-checking, written records, and independent witnesses can reduce noise and sometimes restore lost information. Without such anchors, repeated verbal transmission becomes especially vulnerable to distortion. The classic rumor literature describes this through leveling, sharpening, and assimilation, whereby retellings lose some details, emphasize others, and reshape the story according to expectation, interest, or prior belief. ⇱ A related pattern appears in serial reproduction experiments, where information passed from person to person shows greater loss of original material and greater distortion than information repeatedly reproduced by the same individual. ⇱

A modern extension takes the same problem one step further by treating messages geometrically as embeddings. An embedding is a vector representation that places a word, sentence, or message in a high-dimensional space according to patterns of use and context. If two messages are used in similar contexts and carry similar meanings, their vectors tend to lie near one another. If retelling alters the context, emphasis, or implication of a message, its vector may move farther away. Diachronic word embeddings apply this idea across historical time, and Hamilton, Leskovec, and Jurafsky use them to measure how word meanings change across corpora. ⇱

For whole messages rather than single words, current text-embedding research generalizes the same logic. Sentences, passages, and documents can be embedded as vectors and compared by similarity, although benchmarks such as MTEB show that performance depends strongly on the task rather than on one universally best embedding method. ⇱

For gossip, this makes semantic drift measurable. Successive retellings M****₀, M₁, M₂, … can be represented as a path through semantic space, and distortion can be approximated by the distance between successive representations or by the divergence between distributions of message representations. This approach belongs more to distributional semantics, applied statistics, and computational linguistics than to classical Shannon theory, but it preserves the same basic lesson. Content transmission requires correction mechanisms because circulation alone does not guarantee fidelity.

4. Incentives and Gossip as a Strategic Game with Reputational Payoffs

The previous section treated gossip as a noisy channel, where each retelling may preserve, distort, or reframe the original message. A further question is why people choose to transmit such messages in the first place. Gossip persists with such force because transmission can serve interests. It can inform, bond, warn, punish, entertain, raise status, or damage a rival.

Game-theoretic language becomes useful here because passing on a rumor can be treated as a decision with expected benefits and costs. Foster's taxonomy of gossip functions identifies several motives that can be translated into utility terms, including information exchange, social bonding, status management, influence, and indirect aggression. ⇱

Baumeister, Zhang, and Vohs add the cultural-learning dimension by showing how gossip can turn norm violations and their consequences into memorable stories. ⇱

This means that incentives explain not only why gossip exists but also how rapidly it moves through the network.

Evolutionary models extend this incentive view by showing that gossip can be more than a private indulgence. Pan, Hsiao, Nau, and Gelfand propose an agent-based evolutionary game-theoretic account in which gossip proliferates because it disseminates reputational information and deters selfish behavior, thereby supporting cooperation. ⇱ Their model explicitly combines reputation dissemination and selfishness deterrence, and their simulations show that gossipers can become dominant while cooperation remains high.

This revises the usual moral caricature of gossip. Under certain conditions, gossip can serve as an enforcement mechanism for prosocial equilibria by making defection visible and costly.

The same logic also suggests a strategic interpretation of the stifler compartment in the Daley–Kendall tradition. A stifler need not be only someone who has lost interest in the rumor. The key point is that contact structure alone does not determine rumor spread. In the fixed-probability model, transmission depends mainly on who meets whom. In the incentive-aware model, the same contact network is filtered through changing local incentives. Early in the process, novelty, status, warning value, or bonding can make transmission attractive, so the active-edge field is strong. As more people become informed, novelty falls, redundancy increases, and reputational risk can rise. The payoff balance declines, and spreaders become more likely to stop. The simulation therefore shows how microscopic payoff differences can alter local transmission probabilities and, through them, the shape and speed of the macroscopic cascade.

5. Platform Amplification and Gossip as a Self-Exciting Process

The previous section treated transmission as a decision made by agents. On social platforms, however, that decision often does not occur in a neutral environment. Posts, reposts, comments, and likes are time-stamped, ranked, and redistributed, so each visible action can alter the conditions for later actions. Platform gossip therefore requires a dynamic model of amplification rather than only a network model of contact or a game-theoretic model of incentives. On social media, a rumor can accelerate through public engagements such as reposts, comments, and likes, which increase exposure and make later engagement more likely, rather than through person-to-person transmission alone.

In a social platform model, the events tᵢ may be posts, reshares, retweets, or comments. The kernel 𝜙 represents the fading influence of past activity. A recent repost can raise visibility and trigger further reposts, but its effect decays over time.

This model captures the special feature that a platform rumor can unfold under feedback rather than pass purely along a fixed social graph. Visibility produces engagement, engagement produces more visibility, and the process can generate bursts even when the underlying interest is temporary. Zhao and collaborators' SEISMIC model uses a self-exciting process to predict final retweet counts from early cascade signals. ⇱ Rizoiu and collaborators develop a related framework that separates exogenous promotion from endogenous response. ⇱

At this point, gossip becomes algorithmic. A ranking system can alter the baseline intensity 𝜇(t) by exposing a rumor to new audiences, and it can also alter the effective excitation kernel by making each engagement more visible. In modern social media networks, the platform itself enters the dynamics that determine whether a rumor fades, circulates locally, or breaks into a burst of amplified attention.

6. Machine-Mediated Gossip

The platform model becomes especially consequential once engagement and truth begin to diverge. Vosoughi, Roy, and Aral analyzed large-scale Twitter rumor cascades and found that false news diffused farther, faster, more deeply, and more broadly than true news. They proposed novelty and emotional response as possible mechanisms. ⇱ Their result does not mean that platforms automatically prefer falsehood, but it shows why systems organized around engagement may become vulnerable to content that is surprising, emotionally charged, sensational, or socially contagious. If engagement correlates more strongly with surprise than with truth, ranking systems can amplify the signals that help unreliable claims spread. Germano, Gómez, and Sobbrio formalize this mechanism through an engagement-ranking model that can fuel misinformation and polarization under plausible assumptions. ⇱

Mathematically, algorithmic curation can be treated schematically as an operator 𝒜 acting on the effective contact process. It changes which edges become active, how often they activate, and which audiences are exposed, thereby altering the parameters of the cascade rather than acting from outside it. ⇱

This also clarifies why the term "gossip" has acquired a second life in computer science. In distributed systems, gossip no longer means morally loaded talk about absent people. It means randomized local exchange. Demers and collaborators described epidemic algorithms for replicated database maintenance, in which nodes spread updates through repeated contacts that resemble rumor propagation. ⇱ Karp, Schindelhauer, Shenker, and Vöcking later analyzed push, pull, and push–pull rumor-spreading protocols, showing how quickly randomized local exchanges can disseminate information across a network. ⇱ The moral category disappears, while the algorithmic core remains because local exchange, partial knowledge, stochastic contact, and global dissemination are enough to produce system-wide propagation.

In classical distributed systems, the word "gossip" refers mainly to dissemination. On platforms, ranking systems shape exposure, while gossip protocols show how information spreads through local exchanges once contact occurs. Human gossip shares this dissemination structure, but it also carries meaning, incentives, reputation, emotion, and risk.

Recent machine-learning and AI systems add another level of complexity because they do more than transmit claims. They can infer, generate, summarize, mutate, verify, or hallucinate semantically structured content. ⇱ A language model that emits a plausible but false statement may seed a rumor. A large deployment may lower the cost of producing many such claims, while automated systems can raise the effective baseline intensity 𝜇(t) by injecting content into many channels at once. At the same time, AI can support retrieval, ranking, correction, and fact-checking when it is embedded in controlled verification workflows. The same technology can act as an amplifier, a mutator, or a stabilizer, depending on how it is placed within the system.

The empirical picture remains mixed. DeVerna and collaborators examine how LLM-generated fact-checks affect beliefs about political headlines and show that such interventions can also reduce discernment when the generated checks are inaccurate. ⇱ In the opposite direction, Williams and collaborators show that LLMs can automate elements of election-disinformation operations, thereby lowering the cost of rumor production. ⇱

A deeper risk arises when the system traps us in an epistemic feedback loop. Human text trains models, model output returns to human channels, and human channels may then re-ingest that output as apparent evidence.

A modern theory of gossip therefore cannot treat content, transmission, and generation as separate processes.

In machine-mediated gossip, the distribution of content evolves under both human and artificial generators, while ranking systems reshape the rates at which different claims are viewed, repeated, corrected, or forgotten.

Epilogue: When the Whisper Became a System

Taken together, these models show why gossip cannot be reduced to a single mechanism. A rumor spreads through networks, changes through retelling, responds to incentives, bursts under platform feedback, and now circulates through machine systems that can rank, generate, mutate, and correct claims. Each layer alters the others. Network position affects exposure, exposure changes incentives, incentives shape transmission, transmission changes content, and content returns to the network as evidence, entertainment, warning, or myth.

In this sense, what we call virality is not a mystical phenomenon. It is a regime in which the effective reproduction of a claim exceeds the forces that slow, correct, or exhaust it. That regime may arise from topology, novelty, trust, emotional reward, ranking systems, or machine-generated repetition. A mathematical theory of gossip is therefore not a single equation but a framework for exploring how local exchanges, noisy content, strategic motives, and amplification mechanisms combine into large-scale patterns of social belief.

At its best, a mathematical theory of gossip helps explain which structural conditions make crowds manipulable and which design choices can reduce that manipulability without destroying legitimate communication. It clarifies why false stories can beat true stories in attention markets, and it can identify levers that alter the payoff structure, including norms, sanctions, friction, verification, and algorithmic design. The same equations that describe a rumor cascade may also help describe a correction cascade. The same network centralities that locate super-spreaders may also locate super-correctors.

Human civilization has always depended on shared stories, but those stories now move through systems that can accelerate, rank, and reproduce them at unprecedented scale.

As human communication changed, the whisper did not disappear but acquired servers, algorithms, rankings, and synthetic voices.
